  async function parseMultipart(stream, boundary) {

The O. T. Johnson Building is a historic commercial structure located at 356 S. Broadway in downtown Los Angeles, California, at the corner of West 4th Street. Originally constructed in 1902 as a seven-story office building, it was designed by prominent architect John Parkinson in the Romanesque Revival style, featuring a glazed pressed-brick facade, iron-and-glass ground floor elements, and paired columns at the main entrance. Commissioned by financier and philanthropist Orson Thomas Johnson (1839ГўВҖВ“1916), the building contributed to the early 20th-century commercial development of the Broadway Theater and Commercial District, one of the most intact historic districts in the United States. It is recognized as a contributing resource to the district, which is listed on the National Register of Historic Places.
Over the decades, the building underwent significant alterations, including the removal of upper stories, reducing it to its current two-story height, and covering of original details, which led to its exclusion from a 1998 update to the district's historic designation. On February 5, 2007, a major fire gutted the structure and an adjacent building early in the morning, requiring over 160 firefighters to extinguish the blaze in about 90 minutes; the incident exposed long-hidden features such as large arched windows and fluted columns beneath modern cladding. The fire caused substantial damage to first-floor businesses, including uninsured losses estimated at $100,000, and prompted discussions of demolition. Despite plans for demolition, the building was not razed and has remained vacant and in a state of disrepair since the fire, amid the ongoing revitalization of the Broadway corridor. As of 2024, it stands as a damaged remnant of Los Angeles's early architectural heritage within the district's mix of theaters, shops, and preserved early 1900s facades.

Broadway Theater District
The Broadway Theater District in downtown Los Angeles formed during the city's explosive growth period between 1900 and 1910, evolving from a modest commercial strip into a premier entertainment and retail hub by the 1910s and 1920s. This development mirrored Southern California's broader real estate boom, with Broadway serving as a central artery for vaudeville, live theater, and early film screenings that attracted regional audiences and solidified the area's reputation as Los Angeles' cultural epicenter.[2][3]
At its peak, the district boasted an array of iconic theaters that defined its theatrical legacy. Key venues included the Pantages Theatre, which opened in 1910 as a vaudeville house before transitioning to films; the Morosco Theatre (later renamed the Globe) in 1913, dedicated to dramatic productions; the Rialto Theatre in 1917, a pioneering movie palace; and Loew's State Theater in 1920, known for its grand architecture and blockbuster premieres. These theaters, concentrated along an eight-block stretch of Broadway, hosted performers ranging from touring Broadway acts to local talents, drawing thousands nightly and fostering a lively atmosphere of neon lights and marquees.[3][4][5]
The district's commercial vibrancy was equally prominent, with major shops and department stores anchoring its retail appeal. Hamburger's Department Store, constructed in 1909 and later rebranded as May Company, emerged as a flagship retailer offering luxury goods and drawing shoppers from across the region. Surrounding these were specialized outlets for jewelry, apparel, and dry goods, creating a dense network of commerce that complemented the entertainment offerings and turned Broadway into a one-stop destination for leisure and consumption.[6][7]
Economically, the Broadway Theater District was instrumental in boosting downtown Los Angeles' vitality from 1900 to the 1920s, generating substantial revenue through theater admissions, film premieres, and retail sales that spurred further business investments and urban expansion. This influx supported job creation in hospitality and construction, while the district's allure as a glamour spot enhanced the city's overall economic draw, contributing to a surge in population and infrastructure during the era. The O. T. Johnson Building functions as a contributing property within this historic ensemble.[2][3][7]
History
Construction and Early Use
The O. T. Johnson Building was constructed in 1902 as a seven-story office structure at 356 South Broadway in downtown Los Angeles, standing as one of the tallest buildings on the street at the time.[8] Designed by architect John B. Parkinson, a prominent figure in early 20th-century Los Angeles architecture, the building exemplified the commercial ambitions of the era's developers. Orson Thomas Johnson, a local real estate investor, commissioned the project to capitalize on the burgeoning demand for office space amid the city's expansion.[9]
This construction occurred during a period of rapid growth in downtown Los Angeles around 1900, fueled by the arrival of railroads, oil discoveries, and population influxes that transformed the area from a modest town into a booming metropolis.[10] The building's steel-frame design with brick cladding allowed for its height, supporting multiple floors for professional tenants and contributing to Broadway's emergence as a commercial hub. Initially purposed as an office building, it quickly attracted businesses seeking central locations near the growing theater and retail districts.
One of the first notable tenants was the Automobile Club of Southern California, which opened its headquarters in the building in 1904, occupying space that underscored the era's rising interest in automotive services.[11] This early occupancy highlighted the structure's role in accommodating new industries during Los Angeles's economic surge, with the club remaining a key presence in its formative years.[12]
Mid-20th Century Developments
In 1952, the O. T. Johnson Building was acquired as part of a larger portfolio of downtown Los Angeles properties in a transaction valued at $11 million (equivalent to approximately $130 million in 2024 dollars, adjusted for inflation using the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index).[13] This purchase reflected ongoing investment in the aging commercial core amid post-World War II economic shifts.
As the Broadway Theater District transitioned from its theatrical heyday, the building saw changes in tenancy, with spaces repurposed for office and professional uses rather than retail or entertainment functions.[14] These adaptations aligned with broader patterns in downtown Los Angeles, where upper floors of historic structures increasingly housed administrative and service-oriented businesses.
By the 1950s and 1960s, early indicators of urban declineГўВҖВ”such as suburban migration, freeway expansion, and the popularity of televisionГўВҖВ”diminished the area's vibrancy, reducing the building's role as a prominent commercial landmark.[15]
Late 20th and 21st Century Events
In the early 2000s, the O. T. Johnson Building underwent a complete renovation circa 2005, though specific details of the work remain limited in historical records.[8] By this period, the structure had already been significantly altered, having been reduced from its original seven stories to just two sometime prior to 2007, reflecting broader patterns of adaptive reuse or deterioration in downtown Los Angeles commercial properties.[16]
The building's modern challenges intensified on February 5, 2007, when a major fire broke out at approximately 5 a.m., engulfing the O. T. Johnson Building at 356ГўВҖВ“364 S. Owned by Eli Sasson, president of Sassony Commercial Real Estate Development, for about 30 years at the time, the properties featured active retail on the ground floorsГўВҖВ”housing businesses such as bridal shops, shoe stores, and money transfer servicesГўВҖВ”while the upper levels had long been vacant. Sasson immediately deemed the buildings unsafe for occupancy and announced intentions to demolish them, citing structural instability and uncertainty over rebuilding plans. Architecture and Design
Original Romanesque Features
The O. T. Johnson Building, constructed in 1902 and designed by architect John Parkinson, exemplifies early 20th-century Romanesque Revival architecture in Los Angeles' downtown commercial core.[8] This style, adapted for urban office use, emphasized robust forms and restrained ornamentation, drawing from medieval precedents while incorporating modern construction methods like a steel frame clad in brick.[16] The building's original design contributed to the evolving aesthetic of Broadway, blending solidity with subtle elegance to complement the district's emerging theater and retail landscape.
Key facade elements highlight the Romanesque influence, including paired columns that flanked the main entrance, providing a classical entry motif with vertical emphasis.[16] Fluted columns extended across the facade, adding rhythmic texture and depth to the glazed pressed-brick surface, which offered a durable, subtly reflective finish. Large arched windows dominated the upper stories, with rounded openings framed by heavy brick surrounds that evoked the style's characteristic massiveness and structural honesty.[16] These features created a cohesive elevation, where the interplay of arches and columns fostered a sense of ordered progression upward.
Originally rising seven stories, the building's scale asserted a commanding yet harmonious presence on South Broadway, anchoring the street's early high-rise development amid lower surrounding structures.[8] Its visual impact lay in the facade's shimmering brickwork under sunlight, which enhanced the thoroughfare's historic vibrancy and underscored the Romanesque Revival's role in defining Los Angeles' commercial identity at the turn of the century.[16]
Materials and Construction Techniques
The O. T. Johnson Building was constructed in 1902 as a seven-story brick structure, utilizing a steel skeleton frame that enabled its height while providing structural integrity for commercial use in downtown Los Angeles.[8] The frame was clad with brick interior walls for partitioning and support, contributing to the building's compartmentalized office spaces. The exterior facade featured glazed brick, selected for its weather-resistant qualities and polished appearance that reflected the era's emphasis on durable, low-maintenance commercial aesthetics.[16]
The ground floor employed iron and glass construction, creating expansive storefronts and entries that maximized visibility and natural light for retail tenants along Broadway.[16] This technique, common in early 20th-century urban commercial design, allowed for flexible commercial layouts while integrating the building into the bustling theater district.
Innovative for 1902 Los Angeles, the building's skeleton frame and brick masonry represented advanced fire-resistant construction methods, reducing risks in a growing city prone to urban fires and allowing for taller buildings than traditional load-bearing walls permitted.[16] These choices aligned with contemporary engineering practices that prioritized safety and scalability in high-density areas, setting a precedent for subsequent developments in the Broadway corridor.
Alterations and Modern Modifications
The O. T. Johnson Building experienced substantial physical alterations in the decades following its 1902 construction, including the demolition of its upper five stories, reducing the structure from its original seven-story height to a two-story remnant. This change dramatically altered the building's scale and prominence within the Broadway Theater District. The original facade, characterized by glazed pressed brick, was further obscured by a covering of tattered brown shingles applied to the upper portions, which hid key Romanesque elements such as large arched windows and fluted columns etched into the surface.[16]
In February 2007, a major fire engulfed the building and its adjacent neighbor at 350ГўВҖВ“354 S. Broadway, originating around 5 a.m. and requiring 160 firefighters to contain over 90 minutes. During suppression efforts, crews stripped away the shingle cladding, exposing the underlying brickwork, columns, and other obscured details for the first time in decades and offering a glimpse of the building's historic character. The blaze exploited the structure's pre-1933 construction vulnerabilities, including unreinforced soft mortar, leaving the interior gutted and the exterior heavily damaged.[16]
Post-fire, the building was deemed unsafe and remained vacant, with owner Eli Sasson applying for demolition permits in 2009 that were denied pending a city-required historic assessment and redevelopment plan. As of 2010, it stood as a gutted shell amid the district's revitalization efforts, with exposed but deteriorated original elements visible on the facade and no active renovation underway; Sasson proposed a multi-story replacement featuring ground-floor retail, though no timeline was established.[17] As of 2024, the building remains a vacant, fire-damaged shell, with repeated demolition requests denied due to its contributing status in the historic district, despite ongoing Broadway corridor revitalization.[18]

Contribution to Downtown Los Angeles
The O. T. Johnson Building, completed in 1902 at 356 South Broadway, exemplified the early 20th-century commercial boom in Los Angeles by providing multi-story office and retail space during a period of rapid southward expansion of the city's business district.[19] Prior to 1900, Broadway south of Third Street was primarily residential, but structures like the O. T. Johnson Building contributed to shifting the commercial core southward, transforming the thoroughfare into Los Angeles's principal retail and business artery by 1910.[19] This development was fueled by regional growth across southern California in the 1900ГўВҖВ“1910 decade, with the building's construction alongside contemporaries such as the Trustee Building and O. T. Johnson Building #2 (both 1905) underscoring the surge in investment and urbanization.[19]
As a prominent seven-story office tower on Broadway, the O. T. National Register Status
The O. T. Johnson Building, located at 356 S. Broadway in Los Angeles, was originally listed as a contributing property to the Broadway Theater and Commercial District on the National Register of Historic Places (NRHP) in 1979, under NRHP reference number 79000484. Following a major fire on February 5, 2007, that gutted the structure and removed much of the modern cladding, original architectural details such as large arched windows and fluted columns were exposed.[16] Although initial plans called for demolition, the building was ultimately preserved and renovated as part of the Broadway corridor revitalization efforts in the 2010s.   function fetchPreview(slug) {
    // Return cached if fresh
    var cached = cache[slug];
    if (cached && (Date.now() - cached.ts) < CACHE_TTL) {
      return Promise.resolve(cached.data);
    }
    // Dedupe in-flight
    if (inflight[slug]) return inflight[slug];

    var url = '/api/page-preview?slug=' + encodeURIComponent(slug);
    var p = fetch(url).then(function (res) {
      if (!res.ok) throw new Error(res.status);
      return res.json();
    }).then(function (data) {
      cache[slug] = { data: data, ts: Date.now() };
      delete inflight[slug];
      return data;
    }).catch(function (err) {
      delete inflight[slug];
      return null;
    });

    inflight[slug] = p;
    return p;
  }

  // These mirror the Next.js extractFirstParagraph + cleanup logic exactly.

  function stripInfobox(content) {
    return content
      .replace(/[\s\S]*?/g, '')
      .replace(/\n{3,}/g, '\n\n');
  }

  function stripLeadingImage(content) {
    var cleaned = content.replace(/^\s*!\[[^\]]*\]\([^)]+\)\s*/m, '');
    return cleaned.replace(/^\s*\*[^*]+\*\s*/m, '');
  }

  function isListItem(line) {
    if (/^[*-]\s/.test(line)) return true;
    if (/^\d+\.\s/.test(line)) return true;
    return false;
  }

  function extractFirstParagraph(content) {
    if (!content) return '';
    var clean = stripInfobox(content);
    clean = stripLeadingImage(clean);
    var lines = clean.split('\n');
    var para = '';
    var inPara = false;

    for (var i = 0; i < lines.length; i++) {
      var t = lines[i].trim();
      if (!t && !inPara) continue;
      if (t.indexOf('') === t.length - 3) continue;
      if (
        t.charAt(0) === '#' || t.indexOf('![') === 0 || t.charAt(0) === '|' ||
        t.charAt(0) === '>' || t.indexOf('```') === 0 ||
        t.indexOf('<grok-render') === 0 || isListItem(t)
      ) {
        if (inPara && para) break;
        continue;
      }
      if (!t && inPara) break;
      if (t) { inPara = true; para += (para ? ' ' : '') + t; }
    }

    // Clean markdown artefacts
    para = para
      .replace(/]*\/>/g, '')
      .replace(/\[([^\]]+)\]\([^)]+\)/g, '$1')
      .replace(/\[\]/g, '')
      .replace(/\(https?:\/\/[^)]+\)/g, '')
      .replace(/https?:\/\/\S+/g, '')
      .replace(/\*\*([^*]+)\*\*/g, '$1')
      .replace(/\*([^*]+)\*/g, '$1')
      .replace(/__([^_]+)__/g, '$1')
      .replace(/_([^_]+)_/g, '$1')
      .replace(/`([^`]+)`/g, '$1')
      .replace(/\s+/g, ' ')
      .trim();

    if (para.length > MAX_PARA) para = para.slice(0, MAX_PARA - 3) + '...';
    return para;
  }

  function extractLeadingImage(content) {
    if (!content) return null;
    var withoutInfobox = stripInfobox(content);
    // Find first H1, take body after it
    var h1Match = withoutInfobox.match(/^(#\s+.+)$/m);
    var body = h1Match
      ? withoutInfobox.slice(withoutInfobox.indexOf(h1Match[1]) + h1Match[1].length).replace(/^\n+/, '')
      : withoutInfobox;
    var imgMatch = body.match(/^\s*!\[[^\]]*\]\(([^\s")]+)(?:\s+"[^"]*")?\)/);
    if (!imgMatch) return null;
    return getControlledImageUrl(imgMatch[1]);
  }

  function getControlledImageUrl(src) {
    if (!src) return null;
    if (src.indexOf('./_assets_/') === 0 || src.indexOf('._assets_/') === 0) return null;
    if (src.indexOf('http://') === 0 || src.indexOf('https://') === 0) {
      try {
        var u = new URL(src);
        if (u.hostname === 'assets.grokipedia.com') return src;
      } catch (e) {}
      return null;
    }
    return null;
  }

  function renderPreview(page) {
    var imageUrl = extractLeadingImage(page.content || '');
    var paragraph = extractFirstParagraph(page.content || page.description || '');
    var html = '';

    if (imageUrl) {
      html += '' +
        '<img src="' + escapeAttr(imageUrl) + '" alt="' + escapeAttr(page.title || '') +
        '" class="h-auto max-h-[200px] w-full object-cover" referrerpolicy="no-referrer" ' +
        'loading="eager" decoding="async" onerror="this.parentElement.style.display=\'none\'" />' +
        '';
    }

    html += '';
    html += '' +
      escapeHtml(page.title || '') + '';
    if (paragraph) {
      html += '' +
        escapeHtml(paragraph) + '';
    }
    html += '';
    return html;
  }

  function escapeHtml(s) {
    return s.replace(/&/g, '&amp;').replace(//g, '&gt;').replace(/"/g, '&quot;');
  }
  function escapeAttr(s) { return escapeHtml(s); }

  // ГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖ Positioning ГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖ
  function positionPopover(linkEl) {
    var rect = linkEl.getBoundingClientRect();
    var gap = 8;
    var pw = 400;
    var vw = window.innerWidth;
    var vh = window.innerHeight;

    // Ensure visible for measurement (but don't move off-screen ГўВҖВ” that
    // triggers mouseleave on the popover and causes the close race).
    popover.style.display = 'block';
    var ph = popover.offsetHeight || 200;

    // Horizontal: prefer left-aligned with link, clamp to viewport
    var left = rect.left;
    if (left + pw > vw - 16) left = vw - pw - 16;
    if (left < 16) left = 16;

    // Vertical: prefer below, flip above if no room
    var top;
    if (rect.bottom + gap + ph <= vh) {
      top = rect.bottom + gap;
    } else if (rect.top - gap - ph >= 0) {
      top = rect.top - gap - ph;
    } else {
      top = Math.max(16, vh - ph - 16);
    }

    popover.style.left = left + 'px';
    popover.style.top  = top + 'px';
  }

  // ГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖ Show / Hide ГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖГўВ”ВҖ
  function showPopover(slug, linkEl) {
    // Cancel any pending hide from a previous hover
    if (hideTimer) { clearTimeout(hideTimer); hideTimer = null; }

    activeSlug = slug;
    activeLinkEl = linkEl;
    popover.classList.add('pointer-events-auto');
    popover.classList.remove('pointer-events-none');

    // Show loading state immediately
    inner.innerHTML = renderLoading();
    positionPopover(linkEl);
    popover.style.opacity = '1';

    fetchPreview(slug).then(function (data) {
      // Bail if user already moved away
      if (activeSlug !== slug) return;
      if (!data || !data.found || !data.page) {
        inner.innerHTML = renderNotFound();
      } else {
        inner.innerHTML = renderPreview(data.page);
      }
      // Reposition after content changes height
      positionPopover(linkEl);
    });
  }

  var hideTimer = null;

  function hidePopover() {
    activeSlug = null;
    activeLinkEl = null;
    popover.style.opacity = '0';
    popover.classList.remove('pointer-events-auto');
    popover.classList.add('pointer-events-none');
    // Hide after fade-out transition ГўВҖВ” but cancel if re-shown
    if (hideTimer) clearTimeout(hideTimer);
    hideTimer = setTimeout(function () {
      hideTimer = null;
      if (!activeSlug) popover.style.display = 'none';

    function formatStatus(s) {
      return {'EDIT_REQUEST_STATUS_IN_REVIEW':'In Review','EDIT_REQUEST_STATUS_APPROVED':'Approved','EDIT_REQUEST_STATUS_REJECTED':'Rejected','EDIT_REQUEST_STATUS_IMPLEMENTED':'Approved'}[s] || 'Pending';

    function statusColor(s) {
      var l = formatStatus(s);
      if (l === 'Approved' || l === 'Implemented') return 'bg-green-500/15 text-green-600 dark:text-green-400';
      if (l === 'Rejected') return 'bg-red-500/15 text-red-600 dark:text-red-400';
      if (l === 'In Review') return 'bg-yellow-500/15 text-yellow-600 dark:text-yellow-400';
      return 'bg-gray-500/15 text-fg-tertiary';
    }

    function timeAgo(ts) {
      if (!ts) return '';
      var t = typeof ts === 'number' ? ts : parseInt(ts, 10);
      if (t < 1e12) t *= 1000;
      var d = (Date.now() - t) / 1000;
      if (d < 60) return 'just now';
      if (d < 3600) return Math.floor(d / 60) + 'm ago';
      if (d < 86400) return Math.floor(d / 3600) + 'h ago';
      if (d < 2592000) return Math.floor(d / 86400) + 'd ago';
      return new Date(t).toLocaleDateString();
    }

    function renderEditCard(edit) {
      var card = document.createElement('div');
      card.className = 'border-b border-border-l1 hover:bg-surface-l2/30 transition-colors';

      var type = formatEditType(edit.type);
      var status = formatStatus(edit.status);
      var color = statusColor(edit.status);
      var time = timeAgo(edit.createdAt);
      var section = edit.sectionTitle || '';
      var summary = edit.summary || '';
      var original = edit.originalContent || '';
      var proposed = edit.proposedContent || '';
      var reviewReason = edit.reviewReason || '';
      var isRejected = (status === 'Rejected');
      var isApproved = (status === 'Approved' || status === 'Implemented');

      // Header row
      var html =
        '' +
          '' +
            '' +
              '' +
              '' + esc(type) + '' +
            '' +
            '' + esc(status) + '' +
          '' +
          (section ? 'Section:' + esc(section) + '' : '') +
        '';

      // Original content (red tint)
      html += expandableBlock('Original Text', original, 'bg-red-500/10');

      // Proposed content (green tint)
      html += expandableBlock('Proposed Change', proposed, 'bg-green-500/10');

      // Summary
      html += expandableBlock('Edit Summary', summary, '');

      // Decision rationale
      if (isRejected && reviewReason) {
        html += '' +
          'Rejection Reason' +
          '' + esc(reviewReason) + '' +
        '';
      }
      if (isApproved && reviewReason) {
        html += '' +
          '' +
            '' +
            'Grok Feedback' +
          '' +
          '' + esc(reviewReason) + '' +
        '';
      }

      // Footer: timestamp
      html += '' +
        (time ? '' + esc(time) + '' : '') +
      '';

      card.innerHTML = html;

      // Wire up expand buttons
      wireExpanders(card);

      // Jump-to-section
      var jumpBtn = card.querySelector('[data-jump-section]');
      if (jumpBtn && section) {
        jumpBtn.addEventListener('click', function(e) {
          e.stopPropagation();
          var sid = section.toLowerCase().replace(/[^\w\s-]/g, '').replace(/\s+/g, '-').replace(/-+/g, '-').trim();
          var el = document.getElementById(sid);
          if (el) { el.scrollIntoView({ behavior: 'smooth', block: 'start' }); closeSidebar(); }
        });
      }

      return card;
    }

The Orpheum Theatre at 842 S. Broadway in Downtown Los Angeles opened on February 15, 1926, as the fourth and final Los Angeles venue for the Orpheum vaudeville circuit. Los Angeles is the second largest city in the United States, and the largest in California, with a metropolitan area population of over 12 million. Los Angeles has the largest skyline in California and the West Coast, with over 800 high-rise buildings. Despite being the country's second largest city, Los Angeles ranks fifth in the United States in terms of skyscrapers taller than 492 feet (150 m), with 31 as of 2026, after New York City, Chicago, Miami, and Houston. Los Angeles has two supertall skyscrapers, buildings rising above 984 feet (300 m) in height: Wilshire Grand Center, the tallest building in the city and in California, and the U.S. Bank Tower. Upon its completion in 1989, the US Bank Tower, at 1,018 feet (310 m), remained the tallest building in the city until Wilshire Grand Center was built in 2017 to a height of 1,100 feet (335.3 m).
The history of skyscrapers in Los Angeles began with the 1903 completion of the 13-story Braly Building, which is often regarded as the first high-rise in the city. The Braly building has since been converted from a commercial structure to a residential tower and is now known as the "Continental Building". The growth of Los Angeles' skyline during the early 20th century was hampered by a height restriction imposed in 1904, prohibiting the construction of any building taller than 150 feet (46 m), effectively limiting the height of buildings to 13 stories. An exception was made for Los Angeles City Hall, built in 1928, which dominated the skyline for over three decades. 
In 1957, the city government removed the 150-foot height limit, allowing taller buildings downtown. Los Angeles went through a large building boom that lasted from the early 1960s to the early 1990s, during which most of the city's tallest buildings were completed, including the U.S. Bank Tower, the Aon Center, and Two California Plaza. Skyscrapers built during this period tended to have flat roofs, a result of an ordinance imposed in 1958, to accommodate fire-fighting equipment. The flat-roof ordinance was rescinded in 2014. From the 1990s to 2000s, skyscraper development largely stalled. Few high-rises were completed in the 2000s in contrast to other major American cities. A second construction boom took place from the mid-2010s to the early 2020s with an increasing share of residential development, which saw the downtown skyline expand southwards. 
The majority of skyscrapers taller than 500 ft (152 m) in Los Angeles are located in the northwest of downtown, bounded to the west by Route 110. The rest are mostly in Century City, the Westsides other business district. Between them is a linear skyline that runs east-west through Koreatown and Miracle Mile, surrounding Wilshire Boulevard. Wilshire Boulevard extends west of Century City to Westwood, where the skyline shifts to residential high-rises. There are numerous high-rise clusters outside Wilshire Boulevard, including Century Boulevard by Los Angeles International Airport (LAX), the Hollywood district in central Los Angeles, as well as Warner Center, Encino and Universal City in the San Fernando Valley. More high-rise clusters are found throughout Greater Los Angeles, including in Glendale, Irvine, and Long Beach.

Los Angeles (often referred to by its initials, LA) is the most populous city in the U.S. state of California, and the commercial, financial, and cultural center of Southern California. Romanesque Revival (or Neo-Romanesque) is a style of building employed beginning in the mid-19th century inspired by the 11th- and 12th-century Romanesque architecture. Unlike the historic Romanesque style, Romanesque Revival buildings tended to feature more simplified arches and windows than their historic counterparts.
An early variety of Romanesque Revival style known as Rundbogenstil ("Round-arched style") was popular in German lands and in the German diaspora beginning in the 1830s. By far the most prominent and influential American architect working in a free "Romanesque" manner was Henry Hobson Richardson. In the United States, the style derived from examples set by him are termed Richardsonian Romanesque, of which not all are Romanesque Revival.
Romanesque Revival is also sometimes referred to as the "Norman style" or "Lombard style", particularly in works published during the 19th century after variations of historic Romanesque that were developed by the Normans in England and by the Italians in Lombardy, respectively. Like its influencing Romanesque style, the Romanesque Revival style was widely used for churches, and occasionally for synagogues such as the New Synagogue of Strasbourg built in 1898, and the Congregation Emanu-El of New York built in 1929. The style was quite popular for university campuses in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, especially in the United States and Canada; well-known examples can be found at the University of California, Los Angeles, University of Southern California, Tulane University, University of Denver, University of Toronto, and Wayne State University.

Architectural revivalism is the use of elements that echo the style of a previous architectural era that have or had fallen into disuse or abeyance between their heyday and period of revival. Revivalism, in a narrower sense, refers to the period of and movement within Western architectural history during which a succession of antecedent and reminiscent styles were taken to by architects, roughly from the mid-18th century, and which was itself succeeded by Modernism around the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Notable revival styles include Neoclassical architecture (a revival of Classical architecture), and Gothic Revival (a revival of Gothic architecture). Revivalism is related to historicism.
Western architecture of the 19th century, including Victorian architecture, is an example of Revivalism.
